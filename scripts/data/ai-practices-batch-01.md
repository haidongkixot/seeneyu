## Practice 1: The Café Welcome
**Skill**: eye-contact | **Difficulty**: beginner | **Style**: pixar-3d | **Character**: Maple

**Character**: A friendly young giant panda in a yellow t-shirt and blue jeans, with a warm round face, bright eyes, and a soft natural smile.

**Scene**: Maple walks into a sunlit café and greets three animal friends, locking eyes with each one in turn for two full seconds before sitting down.

**Coaching note**: Notice how Maple holds eye contact for two seconds with each friend before moving to the next. This sweep & hold pattern signals genuine recognition vs perfunctory greeting — every friend feels individually seen, not collectively acknowledged.

### Main Video (15s)
> Pixar-style 3D animation in the visual tone of Kung Fu Panda. Maple, a friendly young giant panda in a yellow t-shirt and blue jeans, walks into a sunlit cozy café with warm color palette and soft golden hour lighting. He sees three animal friends — a rabbit, a fox, and a meerkat — seated at a wooden round table. Beat 1 (0-5s): Maple approaches the table and locks eyes first with the rabbit on the left, holding the gaze with a warm smile. Beat 2 (5-10s): Maple's gaze sweeps right to the fox in the middle, holds for two seconds with a widening smile. Beat 3 (10-15s): Maple completes the sweep to the meerkat on the right, smiles, then waves with both hands and says 'Hey friends!' in a warm English voice. Acoustic café background music with ambient cup-clinking. Medium-wide shot with a slight handheld feel.

### Observation Guide — How Maple earns instant trust with the sweep & hold technique
- **2s** — *Approach Lock*: Maple locks eyes with the first friend before reaching the table. (Establishes connection before proximity, signaling intentionality.)
- **6s** — *Sweep Transition*: His gaze deliberately moves left to right without skipping anyone. (Each friend feels individually seen, not collectively greeted.)
- **10s** — *Two-Second Hold*: Each gaze lock lasts a full two seconds before moving on. (Two seconds is the minimum for the brain to register real attention.)
- **13s** — *Smile-Sync Release*: Smile widens at the moment of eye contact, then voice and hands join in. (Layering eyes + smile + voice + hands creates a full-spectrum welcome.)

### Practice Steps
**Step 1: Approach Lock** (15s)
- **Instruction**: Before you reach the room, pick the first person you'll greet. Lock eyes with them as you walk toward them, holding the gaze for at least two seconds.
- 💡 **Tip**: Don't break gaze until they acknowledge you back.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Pixar 3D close-up: Maple the panda walking toward camera in a sunlit café, eyes locked forward with a warm focused gaze. Five seconds of confident approach, no speech, subtle ambient café sounds.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of a friendly panda character mid-stride, eyes locked forward in a warm gaze, gentle smile, confident posture. White background, no text.

**Step 2: Sweep Transition** (20s)
- **Instruction**: Move your eyes deliberately from one person to the next. Don't skip anyone. The motion should feel intentional, not scanning.
- 💡 **Tip**: Imagine you're painting each face with your gaze before moving on.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Pixar 3D medium shot of Maple's face from chest up. His eyes deliberately move left, hold, then right, hold, then center, hold. Each hold lasts about a second. Soft background blur, no speech.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of a panda character's face in medium shot, eyes mid-transition between left and right, eyebrows relaxed, soft smile. White background, no text.

**Step 3: Two-Second Hold** (20s)
- **Instruction**: When your eyes lock with someone, count one Mississippi, two Mississippi before moving on. Two full seconds is the minimum.
- 💡 **Tip**: If two seconds feels too long, focus on noticing one detail about their face.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Pixar 3D tight close-up of Maple the panda's face, eyes locked directly into camera. He holds the gaze still for five full seconds, blinking softly, with a calm warm expression. No speech.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of a panda's face in tight close-up, eyes locked directly at viewer with a sustained warm gaze. White background, no text.

**Step 4: Smile-Sync Release** (15s)
- **Instruction**: At the moment your eyes lock, let your smile widen by a fraction. Don't smile before — let the eye contact trigger the smile.
- 💡 **Tip**: The smile should arrive a fraction of a second after the eye lock, never before.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Pixar 3D close-up of Maple's face. His eyes lock onto the viewer, hold for one beat, then his smile slowly widens into a Duchenne smile that crinkles the eyes. No speech, soft chime sound.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of a panda character's face mid-smile, eyes already locked with viewer, smile blossoming with crinkled eyes. White background, no text.

## Practice 2: The Power Pause
**Skill**: eye-contact | **Difficulty**: intermediate | **Style**: pixar-3d | **Character**: Coco

**Character**: A tall slender meerkat in a cozy turtleneck sweater, expressive eyes, theatrical hand gestures, the energy of a natural storyteller.

**Scene**: Coco the meerkat is telling a story to two friends around a campfire, then suddenly stops mid-sentence and locks eyes with one listener for a full two seconds before delivering the next line.

**Coaching note**: Notice how Coco breaks the rhythm of speech to create an eye-contact spotlight. The pause + locked gaze tells the listener 'this part is for you.' Most speakers fill silence; the best ones use it as a beam.

### Main Video (15s)
> Pixar-style 3D animation in the visual tone of Madagascar. Coco the meerkat in a cozy turtleneck sweater is sitting cross-legged in front of a small campfire with two friends — a fennec fox and a bushbaby. Warm orange firelight, evening forest setting, gentle crickets in the background. Beat 1 (0-5s): Coco is mid-story, gesturing animatedly with both paws, says 'And then I opened the door and you'll never guess what I saw...' Beat 2 (5-10s): Coco suddenly stops speaking, eyes lock onto the fennec fox, holds the gaze for two full seconds while the campfire crackles. Beat 3 (10-15s): Still holding eye contact, Coco whispers 'A cake. With my name on it.' English voice, warm and conspiratorial. The two friends lean in. Soft acoustic guitar plus crackling fire SFX. Medium two-shot of Coco and fox.

### Observation Guide — How Coco turns silence into a spotlight
- **3s** — *Story Build*: Coco gestures rapidly while building anticipation in the story. (Establishes a baseline rhythm that the pause will break.)
- **6s** — *Hard Stop*: Coco abruptly stops mid-sentence and locks eyes with one listener. (The sudden silence + gaze focuses 100% of attention on the chosen target.)
- **9s** — *Two-Second Hold*: The eye lock is held for a full two seconds without blinking away. (Long enough to feel intentional, short enough not to feel hostile.)
- **12s** — *Whispered Release*: Coco delivers the punchline in a whisper while still holding the gaze. (Drops in volume amplify the closeness already built by the eye contact.)

### Practice Steps
**Step 1: Build Rhythm First** (20s)
- **Instruction**: Start your story at a natural pace with normal eye-contact patterns. The pause only works if there's a rhythm to break.
- 💡 **Tip**: If you pause from a slow start, it just feels like you forgot what to say.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Pixar 3D medium shot: Coco the meerkat gesturing animatedly while telling a story by a campfire. Hands moving, eyes scanning between two listeners. Five seconds of natural storytelling rhythm. English voice but words muffled.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of a meerkat character mid-story, paws raised, mouth open, eyes scanning. White background, no text.

**Step 2: Hard Stop** (15s)
- **Instruction**: Mid-sentence, stop speaking completely. Don't trail off. Pick one listener and lock eyes with them.
- 💡 **Tip**: The cleaner the stop, the bigger the spotlight.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Pixar 3D close-up: Coco's face mid-word, then suddenly her mouth closes and her eyes snap to lock onto a single listener off-camera. The transition is sharp. No speech after the snap.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of a meerkat character with mouth half-open, eyes intensely locked forward. White background, no text.

**Step 3: Two-Second Hold** (15s)
- **Instruction**: Once your eyes lock, hold them there for two full seconds. Resist the urge to break the silence.
- 💡 **Tip**: Count two slow heartbeats before you continue.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Pixar 3D close-up of Coco's face holding a steady eye contact for five seconds straight. Slight blink at second three, otherwise locked. Faint campfire crackle in the background. No speech.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of a meerkat character face in close-up, eyes locked directly at viewer, calm expression, slight forward tilt of head. White background, no text.

**Step 4: Whispered Release** (20s)
- **Instruction**: When you finally speak again, drop your voice slightly and stay locked on the same person. Don't break eye contact while delivering the line.
- 💡 **Tip**: Lowering your voice while keeping the gaze doubles the intimacy.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Pixar 3D close-up: Coco still locked on the listener, then her lips move in a soft whisper. Eyes don't move. English voice, conspiratorial whisper, says 'with my name on it.'
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of a meerkat character whispering, lips slightly parted, eyes still locked forward. White background, no text.

## Practice 3: The Triangle Trick
**Skill**: eye-contact | **Difficulty**: intermediate | **Style**: pixar-3d | **Character**: Rio

**Character**: A bright tropical toucan with an oversized colorful beak, sitting at a coffee shop table, intelligent and attentive eyes.

**Scene**: Rio the toucan is listening to a friend speak across a coffee table. His gaze rotates in a slow triangle — left eye, right eye, mouth — never staring rigidly at one spot.

**Coaching note**: Notice how Rio's eyes never freeze on a single point. The slow triangle (eye, eye, mouth) keeps the gaze warm and engaged without feeling intense. Holding one spot too long reads as challenge; the triangle reads as connection.

### Main Video (15s)
> Pixar-style 3D animation in the visual tone of Coco. Rio the tropical toucan with a vibrant orange-and-yellow beak sits at a small wooden coffee shop table across from a friend (off-camera). Warm cozy lighting, plants in the background, latte steam rising. Beat 1 (0-5s): Tight close-up of Rio's face, his eyes look at the friend's left eye, holding for a beat. Beat 2 (5-10s): His gaze drifts gently to the friend's right eye, holds, then drifts down to the friend's mouth. Beat 3 (10-15s): The triangle continues — back up to the left eye, hold, soft attentive expression throughout. No speech, just soft acoustic café music and ambient murmur. Tight close-up of Rio's face the entire time. Subtle camera push-in over the 15 seconds.

### Observation Guide — How Rio uses the triangle to stay warm without staring
- **2s** — *Left Eye Anchor*: Rio's gaze lands on the friend's left eye and holds for about two seconds. (Anchoring on one eye first feels like genuine focus, not scanning.)
- **6s** — *Slow Drift*: His gaze drifts smoothly to the right eye — no jump, no flick. (Slow drifts read as engaged thinking; fast flicks read as nervous.)
- **10s** — *Mouth Stop*: Gaze drops to the friend's mouth for a beat, then back up. (Looking at the mouth signals you're listening to words, not just observing.)
- **13s** — *Triangle Loop*: The cycle restarts back at the left eye. (The repeating loop maintains active attention without ever locking too long.)

### Practice Steps
**Step 1: Left Eye Anchor** (15s)
- **Instruction**: Start by looking at the speaker's left eye. Hold for about two seconds. Don't move yet.
- 💡 **Tip**: Pick one eye to start — never try to look at both at once.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Pixar 3D tight close-up of Rio the toucan's face, gaze locked on a single point. Five seconds of steady focus, no movement. Soft café sounds, no speech.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of a toucan character's face in close-up, large eyes focused on a single point, calm expression. White background, no text.

**Step 2: Slow Drift to Right Eye** (15s)
- **Instruction**: After two seconds, slowly drift your gaze to the speaker's other eye. The motion should be smooth, not a flick.
- 💡 **Tip**: Think of it as your eye sliding, not jumping.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Pixar 3D close-up of Rio's face, eyes drifting slowly from one position to another over five seconds. Smooth motion, no speech, attentive expression.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of a toucan character's face with eyes mid-drift between two positions. White background, no text.

**Step 3: Drop to Mouth** (15s)
- **Instruction**: Next, let your gaze drop to the speaker's mouth for one or two seconds. This shows you're tracking their words.
- 💡 **Tip**: Don't stay at the mouth too long — it's a stop, not a destination.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Pixar 3D close-up of Rio's face, eyes drop slightly downward for two seconds, then begin to rise. Subtle natural motion, no speech.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of a toucan character with eyes looking slightly downward, attentive expression. White background, no text.

**Step 4: Loop the Triangle** (25s)
- **Instruction**: Cycle back to the first eye and repeat. Each segment of the triangle should last about two seconds.
- 💡 **Tip**: Don't count out loud — the rhythm should feel natural after a few rotations.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Pixar 3D close-up of Rio the toucan, eyes performing a slow triangle pattern: left eye, hold, right eye, hold, mouth, hold, repeat. Ten seconds total of smooth gaze cycling. No speech.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of a toucan character with three small directional indicators showing eye-eye-mouth triangle pattern. White background, no text.

## Practice 4: The Stage Sweep
**Skill**: eye-contact | **Difficulty**: advanced | **Style**: realistic | **Character**: Sara

**Character**: A young woman in her late twenties wearing a smart casual blazer and jeans, calm posture, confident but approachable presence.

**Scene**: Sara stands on a small stage in front of an audience of about thirty people. Before saying her first word, she scans the room with deliberate two-second holds on three different faces — left, center, right.

**Coaching note**: Notice how Sara establishes connection with the entire audience before opening her mouth. The three-point sweep with held gazes makes every section of the audience feel addressed personally. Speakers who skip this step start their talk feeling disconnected from the room.

### Main Video (15s)
> Realistic cinematic short film with warm naturalism and soft natural lighting. Sara, a young woman in her late twenties wearing a navy blazer and jeans, stands on a small wooden stage in front of an audience of about thirty people in a bright modern auditorium. Beat 1 (0-5s): Sara walks to her mark, takes a slow breath, looks at one person in the front-left section, holds eye contact for two full seconds with a soft smile. Beat 2 (5-10s): Her gaze moves smoothly to a person in the center-back, holds for two seconds, eyes warm. Beat 3 (10-15s): Gaze sweeps to the front-right, holds, then she says 'Good morning everyone' in a calm warm English voice. Subtle ambient room tone, no music. Wide shot from side angle showing both Sara and the audience faces. Slow camera push-in over the 15 seconds.

### Observation Guide — How Sara builds room-wide connection before her first word
- **1s** — *Pre-Sweep Breath*: Sara takes one slow breath before looking up at the audience. (The breath resets her nervous system and signals composure.)
- **4s** — *First Anchor*: She picks one specific face on the left and holds it for two seconds. (Looking at one person, not 'the audience,' is what makes the gaze feel real.)
- **8s** — *Center Sweep*: Her gaze moves smoothly to a face at the back-center. (Including the back rows tells everyone they matter, not just the front.)
- **12s** — *Right Anchor*: She holds the right side last, then opens her mouth to speak. (By the time she speaks, every section feels personally addressed.)

### Practice Steps
**Step 1: Pre-Sweep Breath** (15s)
- **Instruction**: Before you look up at the audience, take one full slow breath. Don't rush this — it sets the entire pace of your opening.
- 💡 **Tip**: Breathe in through the nose for three counts before lifting your eyes.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Realistic cinematic medium shot of Sara on stage, eyes lowered, taking one slow deep breath. Five seconds, no speech, soft auditorium ambient sound.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of a young woman in a blazer standing calm with eyes closed, mid-breath. White background, no text.

**Step 2: First Anchor** (15s)
- **Instruction**: Pick one specific face in the left section of the audience. Lock eyes with them for a full two seconds. Don't look at 'the crowd.'
- 💡 **Tip**: If you can't pick a face, your gaze is hitting empty seats — choose a person.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Realistic cinematic medium shot of Sara from over the audience's shoulder, her gaze locked on one viewer in the front-left. Five seconds of held eye contact, no speech.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of a presenter looking deliberately to one side at a single point in the audience. White background, no text.

**Step 3: Smooth Center Sweep** (15s)
- **Instruction**: After two seconds, move your gaze smoothly to a face in the center, ideally toward the back. Hold for two seconds.
- 💡 **Tip**: Smooth means your head can move slightly with your eyes — don't keep your face frozen.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Realistic cinematic medium shot of Sara, head turning gently from left to center, eyes following. Smooth natural motion over five seconds, no speech.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of a presenter mid-turn, gaze in motion from left to center. White background, no text.

**Step 4: Right Anchor & Open** (20s)
- **Instruction**: Complete the sweep on the right side, hold for two seconds, then begin speaking. Your first word should land while your eyes are still locked on the right anchor.
- 💡 **Tip**: Speaking out of an eye lock makes your opening land twice as hard.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Realistic cinematic medium shot of Sara holding eye contact with someone on the right of frame, then speaking 'Good morning everyone' in a warm calm English voice while still locked.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of a presenter looking right with mouth slightly open mid-word. White background, no text.

## Practice 5: The Genuine Smile
**Skill**: facial-expressions | **Difficulty**: beginner | **Style**: pixar-3d | **Character**: Bao

**Character**: A round and gentle koala with soft grey fur, wearing a pastel pink sweater, with bright thoughtful eyes and a mouth that crinkles naturally when she smiles.

**Scene**: Bao the koala is handed a small wrapped gift by a friend. She first reacts with a quick polite smile that doesn't reach her eyes, pauses, then breaks into a real Duchenne smile where her cheeks lift and her eyes crinkle.

**Coaching note**: Notice the difference between the first smile (mouth only) and the second smile (eyes + cheeks + mouth). The Duchenne smile is the only smile humans can't fake on demand — it requires a genuine internal shift. The trick is to think of something that genuinely delights you.

### Main Video (15s)
> Pixar-style 3D animation in the visual tone of Coco. Bao the gentle grey koala in a pastel pink sweater is sitting on a sunlit garden bench. A friend (off-camera) hands her a small wrapped gift. Warm afternoon light, soft greenery in the background. Beat 1 (0-5s): Bao receives the gift, her mouth pulls into a quick polite smile — corners up but eyes neutral. She pauses. Beat 2 (5-10s): A flicker of recognition crosses her face. Her cheeks slowly lift, her eyes start to crinkle, and the smile grows from the inside out. Beat 3 (10-15s): The full Duchenne smile blooms — eyes squinting with joy, cheeks high, she says 'Oh wow, thank you!' in a warm sincere English voice. Soft acoustic ukulele music plus a gentle 'aww' SFX. Tight medium shot of Bao's face throughout.

### Observation Guide — How Bao shows the difference between a polite smile and a real smile
- **3s** — *Polite Smile*: Her mouth corners go up but her eyes stay neutral and unchanged. (This is the smile we make on autopilot — it doesn't activate the eye muscles.)
- **7s** — *Internal Shift*: Her face goes still for a beat as she processes a real feeling. (The Duchenne smile requires a genuine emotion to trigger.)
- **10s** — *Cheek Lift*: Her cheeks rise, pushing the lower eyelids up and crinkling them. (The cheek lift is the visible signature of a real smile.)
- **13s** — *Eye Crinkle*: Her eyes squint slightly into crescent shapes as the smile peaks. (Crinkled eyes are the only part of a smile humans can't consciously fake.)

### Practice Steps
**Step 1: Polite Smile** (15s)
- **Instruction**: Pull only the corners of your mouth up. Keep your eyes still. Notice how empty this feels — that's the polite smile.
- 💡 **Tip**: Look in a mirror — if your eyes don't change, it's a polite smile.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Pixar 3D close-up of Bao the koala's face. Mouth corners pull into a flat polite smile, eyes completely neutral. Five seconds of held expression, no speech.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of a koala character face with a small mouth-only smile, eyes neutral and unchanged. White background, no text.

**Step 2: Trigger a Real Memory** (20s)
- **Instruction**: Think of something that genuinely delights you — a person, a memory, a place. Let the feeling arrive before the smile does.
- 💡 **Tip**: Don't smile first and try to find the feeling — let the feeling come first.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Pixar 3D close-up of Bao's face going still, eyes softening as a memory crosses her mind. Five seconds of subtle internal shift, no speech.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of a koala face in still contemplation, eyes softened as if remembering something pleasant. White background, no text.

**Step 3: Cheek Lift** (15s)
- **Instruction**: Let your cheeks rise on their own as the feeling builds. Don't push them — they should lift because of the emotion underneath.
- 💡 **Tip**: If your cheeks aren't moving, the feeling isn't strong enough yet.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Pixar 3D close-up of Bao's face, cheeks slowly rising, eyes beginning to narrow as a real smile builds. Five seconds, no speech.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of a koala face with cheeks visibly lifted, smile starting to spread. White background, no text.

**Step 4: Eye Crinkle** (15s)
- **Instruction**: Notice your eyes squinting slightly as the smile peaks. This is the Duchenne marker — the involuntary signature of a real smile.
- 💡 **Tip**: If your eyes are wide open during a smile, it's not landing as warm.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Pixar 3D close-up of Bao's face at full Duchenne smile, eyes crinkled into crescents, cheeks high, soft warmth. Five seconds, no speech.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of a koala face in full warm smile with crinkled eyes and lifted cheeks. White background, no text.

## Practice 6: The Curiosity Look
**Skill**: facial-expressions | **Difficulty**: beginner | **Style**: pixar-3d | **Character**: Pip

**Character**: A small playful sea otter with whiskery cheeks and big alert eyes, wearing a striped scarf, the kind of character who always looks like they're about to ask a question.

**Scene**: Pip the otter is listening to a friend share a surprising piece of news. He tilts his head to one side, raises one eyebrow, and leans in slightly, his whole face becoming a question mark.

**Coaching note**: Notice how Pip's curiosity shows up in three layers stacked together: head tilt, eyebrow raise, and forward lean. Each layer alone would be ambiguous; together they unmistakably say 'tell me more.' The forward lean is what makes the curiosity feel inviting rather than skeptical.

### Main Video (15s)
> Pixar-style 3D animation in the visual tone of Madagascar. Pip the playful sea otter in a striped red scarf is sitting on a wooden dock by a calm lake, talking with a friend (off-camera). Soft afternoon light, gentle water reflections. Beat 1 (0-5s): Pip is listening with a neutral attentive expression. Beat 2 (5-10s): His head tilts gently to the right, then his right eyebrow lifts in a single sharp curve. Beat 3 (10-15s): He leans forward slightly, his eyes widen with interest, and he says 'Wait — really?' in a playful curious English voice. Soft acoustic mandolin music plus gentle lake water sounds. Tight medium shot of Pip's face.

### Observation Guide — How Pip stacks three signals to show genuine curiosity
- **3s** — *Neutral Baseline*: Pip starts with a neutral attentive expression — nothing exaggerated. (The curiosity has more impact when it arrives from a calm baseline.)
- **6s** — *Head Tilt*: His head tilts about ten degrees to one side. (The tilt signals his brain is processing something new and interesting.)
- **9s** — *Single Eyebrow Lift*: One eyebrow lifts in a sharp curve while the other stays still. (Asymmetrical eyebrow lift reads as curiosity; both up reads as surprise.)
- **12s** — *Forward Lean*: He leans slightly forward at the same time his eyes widen. (The lean turns the curiosity into an invitation for the speaker to continue.)

### Practice Steps
**Step 1: Neutral Baseline** (10s)
- **Instruction**: Start with a calm, neutral attentive face. No frown, no smile. The curiosity needs to arrive from somewhere.
- 💡 **Tip**: If you're already smiling, the curiosity will look fake.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Pixar 3D close-up of Pip the otter's face in calm neutral attention, eyes soft. Five seconds of held expression, no speech.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of an otter character's face in calm attentive neutral. White background, no text.

**Step 2: Head Tilt** (15s)
- **Instruction**: Tilt your head about ten degrees to one side. Keep the rest of your face still.
- 💡 **Tip**: Don't tilt too far — small movements read as thoughtful, big tilts read as confused.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Pixar 3D close-up of Pip's face slowly tilting ten degrees to the right. Five seconds of held tilt, neutral expression, no speech.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of an otter character with head tilted ten degrees to one side. White background, no text.

**Step 3: Single Eyebrow Lift** (15s)
- **Instruction**: Lift only one eyebrow — the one on the same side as the tilt. Keep the other eyebrow still.
- 💡 **Tip**: Practice in a mirror. If both eyebrows go up, slow down and focus on one.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Pixar 3D extreme close-up of Pip's eyes and forehead, one eyebrow lifting in a sharp curve while the other stays still. Five seconds, no speech.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of an otter character with one eyebrow visibly raised. White background, no text.

**Step 4: Forward Lean** (15s)
- **Instruction**: Lean your upper body slightly forward — about ten degrees. Pair it with widening your eyes by a fraction.
- 💡 **Tip**: The lean turns curiosity into 'tell me more' — without it you might look skeptical.
- 🎬 **Video prompt (5s)**: Pixar 3D medium shot of Pip the otter leaning slightly forward, head tilted, eyebrow raised, eyes wide. Says 'Wait — really?' in a playful curious English voice.
- 🖼  **Image prompt**: Minimalist sketch of an otter character leaning slightly forward, head tilted, one eyebrow raised, eyes wide with interest. White background, no text.

## Practice 7: The Empathy Mirror
